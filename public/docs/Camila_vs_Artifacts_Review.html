Camila's Finding · Five Things to Define First
Define What You Sell Before
You Design How to Sell It
Client/service types → Ownership → Billing → Financial impact → Response times. In that order. Nothing else makes sense until this is done.
Camila Said
"We need to be so clear that someone with zero knowledge of what you do can graph these out and explain it to anyone."
Segment client categories. Inventory all services. Standardize offerings into clear products. Define subscription tiers and scope inclusions. Then assign ownership, billing model, financial impact, and SLAs — in sequence. Everything downstream depends on getting this foundation right first.
The Artifacts Say
Partially Addressed
The Client Profitability Analysis and 2026 Budget have the financial impact layer (her item 4) already built. The two-track structure partially answers the ownership question (item 2). But items 1, 3, and 5 don't exist as documents. There is no clean, one-page grid of what Wonderly sells, to whom, at what price structure, with what included/excluded. The financial model assumes those definitions — it doesn't produce them.
Verdict
Alignment
Her sequencing is exactly right. The artifacts confirm the financial layer exists — but the definitional layer upstream doesn't.
What's Missing
A service + subscription definition document. One page: service lines, client types, what's included, what's not, billing model per line, FOH owner per type.
Action for Friday
Adam and Allie answer Camila's five questions together. Allie records a Loom showing how she quotes. Adam shares the pricing spreadsheets. Camila converts it into the definition document.

Camila's Finding · Client Lifecycle
Offboarding Doesn't Exist.
What You Need Is a DTR.
"Rather than an offboarding process, it's a DTR process — Define The Relationship. Concierge tells the client what phase they're entering, what they're paying for, how to communicate."
Camila Said
"Production finishes and emails the client. Two weeks are up and they're still asking for things that we should be saying, of course we can help you, but it's going to cost you."
The DTR moment: concierge confirms the client is transitioning from production to maintenance. Sends a clear summary of what they purchased, what they have access to, what Wonderly expects for communication, and what falls outside scope going forward. Cross-sell opportunity lives here too.
The Artifacts Say
Gap — Not in Artifacts
The Content Marketing document maps the service experience from first contact through ongoing partnership — but the transition between phases is missing. Launch → Ongoing Partnership has no named handoff moment. The Pasta Queen's scope creep problem is a direct consequence of this gap: the retainer continued without a DTR that reset expectations when the work expanded.
Verdict
Alignment
This is new and correct. No artifact anticipated it.
What to Add
Insert the DTR as a named stage between Launch and Ongoing Partnership in the service experience system. It belongs to the account lead and is mandatory at every phase transition.
Action
Update the Content Marketing document. Add DTR to the service moments timeline. Brief Marin on running it first on the non-creator track where it's most urgently needed.

Camila's Finding · The Sharpest Insight
For Creator Clients, Wonderly
Is Part of Their Product.
"For niche clients, our product is part of their product. For others, our product is a vehicle for their product. That's why the perceived value is different."
Camila Said
"For the Pasta Queen, without our design, whatever she's selling will not be sold. We're bringing her persona to life. But for Aunt Flow, our product is just a vehicle to sell their product."
This distinction explains why involvement depth, pricing ceiling, account lead personality, revision tolerance, and service model are fundamentally different between the two tracks — not just operationally different but philosophically different. The niche client is buying Wonderly's identity participation. The non-creator client is buying reliable execution.
The Artifacts Say
Sharper Than Artifacts — Updates Them
The Org Brief names two tracks and their different account leads. The Brand Intensity Audit describes two voice registers. But neither document articulates why the tracks are different at a philosophical level. Camila's framing — ingredient vs. vehicle — is the clearest explanation of the two-track thesis in this entire project. It has pricing, scoping, staffing, and relationship implications that should be formally encoded.
Verdict
Alignment
This doesn't just align with the artifacts — it improves them. It's the philosophical foundation the two-track model was missing.
Where It Should Go
Org Brief (why the tracks exist), Founder Team Brief (how to explain the tracks to new hires), Brand Intensity Audit (why the voice registers differ). It belongs in all three.
Action
Encode this framing formally. "Ingredient vs. vehicle" should become the internal language for the two-track distinction going forward.

Camila's Finding · Culture
The Team Has Zero Financial
Consideration in Daily Decisions.
"Maria spent 8 hours on rework for the Pasta Queen. That's making us lose money. But it's not in any consideration from anyone — because it's just something we don't talk about."
Camila Said
"Quality is the first bottom line. But number two, you have to put time into consideration. Because at the end of the day, if we're not hitting our financial targets, then what are we doing?"
Triple bottom line thinking: quality + time + financial impact. Not "go faster." But "understand that one more hour impacts everyone across the organization, not just your project." This needs to become a cultural norm — not just a management metric — once unit economics are understood.
The Artifacts Say
Addressed — But Not Yet Lived
The scorecards include hours-to-estimate accuracy and project margin for Matt and Marin. The Zingerman's transparency roadmap in the Growth Plan opens the books by Q3 2026. The brief discipline in the Operating Brief is the structural mechanism — a complete brief before Figma opens protects margin from the front. But Camila correctly identifies that the culture hasn't absorbed this yet. The structure is there. The belief isn't.
Verdict
Alignment
Strong structural alignment. Camila is naming the cultural gap that the artifacts haven't closed yet — and won't close until the books open and briefs are enforced.
Connection to Make
When the brief system rolls out, explicitly connect brief compliance to margin protection. Maria's rework example is the case study to use. Name it by name. Make the cost visible.
Action
Include financial impact framing in the brief discipline rollout. Not as a threat — as shared context. "A complete brief is how we protect everyone's time, including our own."

Camila is ahead of the artifacts here. Her read is harder and more specific — and it aligns with what Adam and Allie both said in the call.
