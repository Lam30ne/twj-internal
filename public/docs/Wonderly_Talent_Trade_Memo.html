Talent & Trade —
How We Talk About
Our Two Types of Clients

A short explainer on a new internal framework you'll start hearing. It's not complicated — but it makes a lot of things clearer.

We've been doing something for years that we never had clean language for. We work with two fundamentally different kinds of clients — and while we always knew the difference intuitively, we didn't have words for it. That made it harder to talk about, plan around, and divide up the work.

Starting now, we're naming them. Internally, our two client types are Talent and Trade.

Track One
Talent
Creators, podcasters, authors, chefs, IP companies, talent agencies — people whose name or persona is their business.

Track Two
Trade

The Core Difference

For Talent clients, our work is part of their product.
For Trade clients, our work is a vehicle for their product.

A Talent client like Molly Baz — remove Wonderly's involvement and her brand is diminished. We're building her world with her. A Trade client like Aunt Flow — they have a clear product and a clear goal. We build the infrastructure that gets them there. Both matter enormously. Neither is more creative or more important. They just need different things from us.

Questions You Might Have
Do clients know which track they're on?
No — and they don't need to. Talent and Trade are internal language. Every client gets the same Wonderly standard of warmth and quality. The tracks help us organize how we serve them behind the scenes.
What if a client doesn't clearly fit one track?
Ask: is this person's identity the product, or do they have a business objective the work serves? That usually settles it. When genuinely unsure, ask Allie or Marin — that's what account leads are for.
Is one track more important or more creative than the other?
No. Talent clients are often where our creative identity is most visible. Trade clients are often where our most durable, highest-margin work lives. We need both. The tracks aren't a hierarchy — they're a recognition that different clients need fundamentally different things from us.
Does this change how I do my work today?
Not immediately. This is language, not a process overhaul. Over the coming months you'll see it show up in ClickUp, in how briefs are structured, and in how we talk about clients in team meetings. For now, just learn the terms and start applying them to clients you already know.
Where can I see which track each client is on?
The Client Mix spreadsheet has every active client tagged with their track. If something looks off, flag it to Marin or Allie. We'll be updating ClickUp to reflect this as Camila's systems work progresses.

That's it. Two tracks, two names, one Wonderly. The work you do every day doesn't change. What changes is that we have cleaner language for it — which makes it easier to talk about, easier to delegate, and easier to build on.

Questions? Bring them to your next 1:1 or drop them in Slack.
