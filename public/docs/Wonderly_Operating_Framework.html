Section 01

What Wonderly Actually Sells

Wonderly sells design thinking and trusted client relationships — not development hours. Development is the production infrastructure that makes the design real.

This distinction shapes everything: how we hire, how we price, and what we hold internally versus source externally. Our design team and our leadership are the product. The client experience is the differentiator. Development is the delivery infrastructure that has to work reliably so the people who define Wonderly can do what only they can do.

The restaurant analogy we use internally is exactly right. We operate like a high-end restaurant with an open kitchen. Front of House delivers clarity, care, and seamless experience. Back of House creates excellence. And when BOH expertise elevates the moment, they step forward — the experience always feels thoughtful, elevated, and personal.

Everything in this document is oriented around protecting and amplifying that core truth as we grow.

Section 03

The Development Model:
Three Tiers, Two Non-Negotiables

The development team operates in a three-tier model that emerged from our internal Shopify framework and applies equally to WordPress. These tiers describe cognitive complexity, not quality level. Every tier operates to the same communication and quality standard.

I
Oversight
Matt — Dev Principal

Holds the client relationship at the project level. Sets quality standards across all dev work. Guides workflows and is the escalation point for Lead developers. Does not execute builds day-to-day — this is a deliberate upgrade to Matt's role, not a reduction. His mastery becomes directional.

II
Lead Developer
Alyssa — WordPress Lead · Jerrod — Shopify Lead

Critically thinks about what gets built and how. Breaks complex requests into executable plans. Owns QA triage, interprets client feedback, leads dev review calls, and makes go/no-go calls on quality. Alyssa translates business problems into technical approaches within WordPress and Beaver Builder. Jerrod does the same within Shopify. Both escalate to Matt for account-level decisions.

III
Production Developer
White-label partner (Nakul's team) → future internal hire

Executes builds to spec within defined parameters. Handles page builds, block builds, configuration, CMS changes, Postmark setup, plugin configuration, and repeatable setup tasks. Works under Alyssa's or Jerrod's direction from a complete brief. Does not own quality decisions — but operates to the same quality standard. This is the role Wonderly currently needs to fill externally and eventually bring in-house.

✦
Communication

Every tier is expected to communicate clearly, proactively, and in plain language. Silence is never acceptable. Response time is a quality signal, not a preference.

✦
Quality

Every tier executes to the same standard. The tiers describe complexity of thinking, not level of care. A Production developer who ships sloppy work is not a junior problem — it's a values problem.

Section 05

The Growth Vision:
From $1M to $3M

The recurring revenue model — builds that transition into hosting, maintenance, retainers, SEO, content marketing, and newsletter growth — is not only possible, it's the natural extension of what clients already trust Wonderly to do. The pathway is three phases, each with a clear unlock condition.

Now → $1.5M · The Assembly Phase

Build the infrastructure that holds weight

Get the three-tier dev structure operational. Alyssa and Jerrod in their Lead roles. Nakul's team flowing production tasks. Camila's COGS analysis complete. Briefs standardized. Marin owning the PM layer. Taylor owning design. Matt directing rather than executing. The goal of this phase is to make the layers below Adam and Allie load-bearing enough that it's safe to let go.

$1.5M → $2M · The Trust Phase

Let the layers hold — and build the recurring offer

Adam and Allie step back from production decisions. The marker: a project runs start to finish without a principal making a build decision. First recurring revenue products are packaged and priced — maintenance plans, hosting tiers, a growth retainer. PM playbook exists and a second PM is on the horizon. A second Lead developer is being evaluated.

$2M → $3M · The Product Phase

Recurring revenue becomes a real business line

Hosting, maintenance, SEO, content marketing, and newsletter growth are packaged products sold at close — not afterthoughts. Adam and Allie are primarily in strategy, new client relationships, and service direction. Camila is running operations. Dev can handle parallel builds without Matt in every one. The business is no longer dependent on principal time to deliver.

Section 06

Where We Will Feel Friction

These are not failure points — they're known pressure zones. Naming them now means leaders can act with clarity when they feel resistance, rather than being surprised by it.

Feels it first · $1M → $1.5M

Principals still too close to production

Adam and Allie are beloved by clients and naturally reach into every layer. The shift to directing rather than building is not automatic — it requires the layers below to be trustworthy enough that it feels safe to let go. This is the central unlock for all growth.

Blocks pricing decisions · Now

COGS and utilization math is not yet defensible

Without knowing the true cost to deliver a service, pricing is guesswork. The recurring revenue model especially requires clear margin math. This is Camila's highest-leverage early project and a prerequisite for packaging any retainer product.

Compounds with every new partner · Now

The brief problem is a hidden scaling tax

Every project that reaches a developer without a complete brief creates rework, delay, and client anxiety. Right now this is managed through proximity and context. It breaks immediately at scale or with external partners. Standardizing the brief is urgent.

Hits hard at $1.5M+

PM layer is a team of one

Marin has the right instincts and leadership. But at $3M, PM is a function that needs 2–3 people operating from the same playbook. Building that playbook now — before volume makes it impossible to step back — is the right move.

Required before $1.5M

Recurring revenue is a service, not a product yet

Wonderly can provide maintenance, SEO, and content marketing — but these don't yet exist as defined products with pricing, scope, and delivery standards. The transition from "we can do that" to "here's our maintenance plan" is a packaging project that unlocks an entire revenue layer.

Grows with every distributed hire

Cultural integration has no formal system

Team rituals, FOH/BOH philosophy, quality standards — these transmit informally at 10 people and stop transmitting at 25. Participation in team culture needs to be an explicit expectation in hiring and a designed part of onboarding, not assumed from proximity.

Now — Tori is early

Marketing team is not yet integrated into the delivery model

Tori joined full-time in February and leads a capability that is central to the recurring revenue vision. But marketing doesn't yet have a defined place in the project handoff flow — when does it engage, what does a brief look like, how does it coordinate with design and dev? Getting this right early prevents the same brief and routing problems dev is solving now from reappearing in a different team.

Section 07

Operating Principles for Leaders

These principles are designed to give team leaders the clarity to make decisions autonomously — without needing to escalate everything to the principals. When in doubt, return to these.

1
Hire for the tier, not the resume

A Lead developer and a Production developer are different cognitive profiles, not the same person at different seniority levels. The job description must be honest about which brain the role needs. Brilliant executors may be poor at triaging ambiguity — and vice versa.

2
Communication is evaluated, not assumed

How a contractor or candidate communicates before they're hired tells you how they'll communicate when a client is waiting. Build this into every evaluation. Silence under pressure is a disqualifier at any tier.

3
Cultural participation is a real expectation

For a distributed, multicultural team, connection is built asynchronously — through team rituals, check-ins, and shared moments. Participation in these is not optional. Surface it clearly in hiring so the right people self-select in and others self-select out before week eight.

4
In-house vs. outside-house is a strategic choice

Some capabilities belong inside the culture because they're core to what Wonderly promises. Others can be sourced externally because they're transactional and the quality can be spec'd and verified. Review this quarterly. Don't let it default by accident.

5
The brief is the standard of care

A complete brief is an act of respect for the person who has to execute it. Sending incomplete direction downstream is not a minor inefficiency — it compounds through rework, delay, and client anxiety. Brief quality is a leadership responsibility, not an admin one.

6
Sales is service — and it starts at project close

In the spirit of the Wonderly sales philosophy: every project close is an opportunity to generously invite a client into an ongoing relationship. Not pressure — an offering extended when trust is highest. Recurring revenue is earned in the build, not sold afterward.

7
When the layers work, the principals can lead

The measure of a healthy structure is when Adam and Allie can focus on what only they can do: strategy, relationships, vision, and business development. Every improvement to the layers below them is an investment in that freedom — and in the company's ability to grow.
